# Embodiedness as Interface

## How Region, Ground, and Percept become alive in Chiasm

Chiasm does not begin from the belief that an image is a container of information waiting to be extracted. It begins from a more difficult claim: an image is a field in which something becomes available to perception. A fold catches the light. A figure leans away from the frame. A patch of red acquires an almost bodily pressure. An empty interval holds two forms apart and, by holding them apart, binds them more intensely than contact would.

These are not merely objects in the image. They are ways in which the image solicits the viewer.

Our architecture already possesses the beginnings of a language for this encounter: **Region, Ground, Percept, Mention, Reading**. Yet these objects do not succeed because their schemas are rigorous. A Region can be perfectly normalized, stored, indexed, and retrieved while remaining perceptually dead. A Percept can have a name, an embedding, and twenty relations while never becoming something the user can actually feel, recognize, manipulate, or summon during writing.

The decisive problem is therefore not extraction. It is **embodiedness**.

Embodiedness here does not mean simulating a human body or decorating the interface with gestures. It means giving every perceptual object a lived mode of existence inside the system: a way of appearing, attracting attention, being approached, changing under action, withdrawing without vanishing, and returning at the moment it becomes meaningful again. The success of Region, Ground, and Percept depends on this dynamic. Without it, the backend possesses a taxonomy. With it, Chiasm begins to possess a perceptual world.

## The percept is not a label

A label says: **sleeve**.

A percept says: **the sleeve loosens the authority of the silhouette**.

The first classifies a visible part. The second gathers a visible part, a quality, a relation, and an effect into one reusable unit of sense. It does not float free of the image, but neither is it imprisoned inside a bounding box. It can return beside a sentence, connect two distant images, become a structural force in the Atlas, or appear as a faint pressure beneath a word in the Codex.

This is why a Percept cannot be reduced to segmentation. Segmentation gives us addressability: *where*. A perceptual system must also hold *how*, *between what*, *under which conditions*, and *with what felt consequence*.

The Region is thus not the final object. It is an aperture through which the user can enter the field. The Ground is not a passive background. It is the condition that makes a particular appearance possible. The Percept is the portable achievement of that encounter.

We can express the movement simply:

> **Ground conditions appearance → Region gives it an address → interaction discloses a quality or relation → Percept preserves the disclosure → Mention recalls it in another context.**

This is the living spine of Chiasm.

## Grounds: the different ways an image can hold meaning

Not every visual fact has the same geometry. If Chiasm forces every experience into a box or polygon, it will recognize objects while mutilating atmosphere, rhythm, tension, and light. The Field therefore needs several kinds of ground, each with its own render and recall behavior.

| Ground | What it holds | How it renders in the Field | How it recalls elsewhere |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Figural ground** | A bounded thing or part: face, cuff, window, chair, garment panel | Contour, soft mask, or restrained outline; solid only on focus | Returns as a crop, silhouette chip, or small highlighted thumbnail |
| **Material ground** | Texture, surface, grain, weave, gloss, brushwork | Texture-preserving magnification, grazing-light overlay, or detail lens | Returns as a tactile swatch with the source crop and a material phrase |
| **Luminous ground** | Illumination, shadow, glow, reflected colour, temperature | Heatmap-like wash, gradient veil, or isolatable light field—not a hard boundary | Returns as a miniature light-map, colour trace, or ambient edge around text |
| **Spatial ground** | Depth, interval, scale, negative space, enclosure, direction | Depth bands, vanishing vectors, translucent planes, or measured gaps | Returns as a spatial diagram or relational preview rather than an object crop |
| **Relational ground** | The tension or affinity between two or more regions | Connective arc, shared halo, elastic band, or activated interval | Returns as a paired/multi-crop card with the relation named between them |
| **Kinetic ground** | Implied movement, gesture, drape, gaze, repetition, visual path | Flow line, directional pulse, sequential trace, or animated scrub | Returns as a short path glyph, ordered crops, or motion phrase beside prose |
| **Compositional ground** | Balance, rhythm, density, hierarchy, framing, distribution | Whole-image scaffold: axes, masses, rhythm marks, focal weights | Returns as a small composition map that preserves the whole rather than a fragment |
| **Atmospheric ground** | Mood distributed across the image: hush, menace, tenderness, estrangement | Low-opacity field wash affecting the whole pane, with intensity control | Returns as a tonal aura around a passage or as a global image-state card |
| **Temporal ground** | Before/after, recurrence, transformation across images or frames | Filmstrip, state slider, ghosted comparison, or aligned sequence | Returns as a time-linked stack showing what persisted and what changed |
| **Conceptual ground** | An interpretation anchored in visible evidence: restraint, theatricality, exposure | Evidence constellation linking the claim to its supporting grounds | Returns as a thesis chip that can unfold back into all visual evidence |

These are not ten unrelated annotation tools. They are ten modes by which the Field can answer a single question: **what kind of visual condition is carrying this percept?**

That distinction matters because rendering is already interpretation. A hard box tells the user that the thing is bounded. A heat field says it is distributed. A line says it moves. A bridge says it exists between. A whole-image veil says it cannot be localized without being destroyed. The UI must not merely display stored ground; it must render the ontology honestly.

## Rendering is the first act of embodiment

For a percept to live, its visualization must be immediately legible without turning the image into a forensic dashboard. The Field should remain an image first. Ground must surface with restraint, intensity, and timing.

The basic interaction should follow four states:

1. **Latent** — the ground exists but does not clutter the image. A faint trace, edge tick, or small margin signal indicates availability.
2. **Soliciting** — proximity, gaze, cursor, selection, or writing context causes the ground to become perceptually noticeable.
3. **Present** — the user focuses it; the correct rendering mode becomes clear, vivid, and manipulable.
4. **Sedimented** — the ground withdraws again, but leaves a compact percept token, history trace, or relation that can be recalled.

This is crucial. Permanent overlays create noise; invisible metadata creates death. Embodied UI lives between the two. It knows how to appear and how to retreat.

Catchiness should not mean spectacle. It should mean **recognitional force**: the user immediately understands that a violet wash is atmosphere, a paired halo is relation, and a pulsing line is movement. Each ground family needs a stable visual grammar—colour family, edge behavior, motion behavior, and miniature form—so that recognition becomes bodily and fast. The user should not repeatedly decode a legend.

Nor should the system open a form before it opens perception. A user encountering a striking fold should first be able to touch, circle, trace, or hold it. Naming may follow. The interface should tolerate preconceptual capture: *this*, *here*, *between these*, *moving this way*, *spread across the scene*. Only afterward should the system offer language, related percepts, or analytic lenses.

This reverses the usual annotation logic. We do not demand that the user know what the thing is before preserving what it does.

## Recall is not search; it is renewed presence

The central danger is archival burial. A percept may be created beautifully and then vanish into a sidebar, a database, or a tag filter. Technically saved, phenomenologically lost.

Recall must therefore be designed as a second embodiment. It should not merely locate the past object; it should restore enough of its original visual force for the user to recognize why it mattered.

Every Percept needs a compact **recall form** composed from five elements:

- a visual nucleus: crop, map, relation pair, path, or whole-field miniature;
- a percept phrase: not only the noun, but the disclosed action or quality;
- its ground signature: figural, luminous, relational, atmospheric, and so forth;
- its source trace: the image and precise evidence from which it arose;
- its resonance: related images, passages, percepts, and structural nodes.

The recall form should be small enough to travel everywhere and rich enough to unfold. In its collapsed state it may sit beside a sentence like a bright seed. On hover or focus it restores the visual nucleus. On activation it opens the Field at the original ground. The movement must be reversible:

> **word → Percept → ground → source image**, and back again.

This reversibility is what prevents language from severing itself from vision.

Recall should also be contextual rather than merely requested. While the user writes about “severity,” the system may quietly surface the earlier percept *the collar disciplines the softness of the cloth*. While an Atlas node gathers images around concealment, Chiasm may recall luminous grounds in which faces disappeared into shadow. These suggestions must inhabit a Perceptual Margin, not invade the sentence. They appear as a restrained peripheral offer: present enough to be caught, quiet enough to be ignored.

Good recall has timing. Too early, it interrupts formation. Too late, it becomes conventional search. The interface should learn three moments:

- **recognition** — the current word or visual action directly matches a known percept;
- **resonance** — a less literal percept could deepen or complicate the thought;
- **return** — an earlier percept from the same work has gone dormant and now becomes structurally important again.

## The Percept as a portable object of thought

To travel through Chiasm, the Percept needs one stable identity but several presentations. It is not copied into every surface. It is cited, unfolded, and rendered according to context.

A useful internal shape is:

- **Identity** — stable `percept_id` and authorship/provenance.
- **Claim** — the concise percept phrase.
- **Grounds** — one or more evidence-bearing visual grounds.
- **Effect** — what the visible arrangement does perceptually or affectively.
- **Relations** — reinforces, contradicts, transforms, echoes, precedes, contains.
- **Recall assets** — miniature/crop/map/path and display behavior.
- **Mentions** — appearances in blocks, words, Atlas nodes, readings, and other percepts.
- **Confidence and openness** — whether this is observed, inferred, contested, or deliberately unresolved.

This gives the project a crucial separation:

> A **Region** stores addressable visual evidence. A **Ground** describes the mode in which that evidence operates. A **Percept** gathers evidence into portable sense. A **Mention** installs that sense inside a new context without duplicating it. A **Reading** composes several Percepts into an argument or encounter.

Once this separation is maintained, the same Percept can exist throughout the system without becoming flattened.

In the **Field**, it is a manipulable appearance.

In the **Writer**, it is a recallable visual thought.

In the **Codex**, it can persist across chapters, gathering new mentions and transformations.

In the **Atlas**, it becomes a node or an active force between nodes.

In **Aletheia**, it becomes grounded evidence for interpretation.

In **Anuraṇana**, it becomes part of the user’s evolving taste and resonance space.

In search, it is retrieved not only by name but by visual, relational, atmospheric, and semantic similarity.

## Percepts within words

The deepest integration occurs when the visual does not merely accompany writing but remains available from within language itself.

A word or phrase in the Writer should be able to carry a **Percept Mention**. The mention is not a hyperlink pasted onto prose. It is a seam in the sentence through which the source image can return.

The sentence might read:

> The silhouette acquires its authority through restraint.

“Authority through restraint” may carry a Percept Mention. At rest, nothing disrupts reading except perhaps a subtle underline, margin glint, or percept glyph. When approached, the garment’s silhouette returns as a miniature; the relevant contour appears, then the light and spatial grounds that supported the claim. The reader can move backward from abstraction to evidence.

Mentions can take several forms:

- **word-bound** — attached to a phrase;
- **block-bound** — governing a paragraph or story block;
- **margin-bound** — available beside the prose without marking its wording;
- **structural** — attached to a chapter, sequence, or Atlas relation;
- **latent** — machine-suggested but not yet accepted by the user.

This is where Chiasm decisively ceases to be another block editor. A conventional editor treats prose as the primary substance and media as attachment. Chiasm allows a sentence to remain answerable to the perceptual event from which it emerged. Words do not merely describe images; they retain passages back into them.

## A product plan for actualising embodiedness

### Phase I — Establish the ground grammar

Begin with a small but ontologically distinct set: **figural, luminous, relational, kinetic, compositional, atmospheric**. These cover bounded things, distributed conditions, between-ness, movement, whole-image organization, and mood. Do not launch ten weak tools. Establish six convincing modes with consistent interaction and visual signatures.

For each mode, define:

- capture gesture;
- Field rendering;
- compact miniature;
- editable properties;
- keyboard and touch behavior;
- accessible non-colour cue;
- recall behavior in the Writer;
- graceful fallback when the ground cannot be precisely computed.

The success criterion is not annotation count. It is whether a user can create a ground, leave it, encounter it later, and immediately recognize what kind of experience was preserved.

### Phase II — Introduce Percept composition

Allow one or several grounds to be gathered into a Percept. The interface should propose a phrase but never force premature naming. A percept composer can ask only three sharp questions:

1. **What is happening here?**
2. **What visible ground makes you say that?**
3. **What does it do to the image or to you?**

The result becomes a portable Percept card with a stable visual nucleus. Automatic regions, creator marks, and Aletheia readings can all feed this composer, but the Percept remains distinguishable from its ingredients.

### Phase III — Build the Perceptual Margin

Place recall beside writing. It should surface percepts from the current image first, then the current collection, then the wider corpus. Ranking should combine:

- explicit links and recent use;
- visual similarity through region embeddings;
- semantic proximity to the current passage;
- structural relevance in the Atlas/Codex;
- productive difference, so recall does not become an echo chamber.

The Margin needs visible reasons: *same luminous structure*, *earlier in this chapter*, *contrasts with this silhouette*, *frequently connected in your readings*. Retrieval without an intelligible reason feels arbitrary; intelligible recall teaches the user their own archive.

### Phase IV — Make Mention reversible

Enable drag, slash command, or direct phrase selection to bind a Percept to words or blocks. Every Mention must open the Percept, every Percept must reveal its grounds, and every ground must reopen the source Field in context. This round trip is the core integrity test.

### Phase V — Let percepts accumulate history

A Percept should not be frozen at creation. Across new images and passages it may be confirmed, complicated, contradicted, or transformed. Preserve these changes as a lineage rather than silently overwriting the original. The user should be able to see that *ornament as excess* later became *ornament as defensive surface*.

This gives the Codex philosophical memory and gives Anuraṇana something more valuable than preference prediction: a history of how the user’s seeing changes.

## Product principles

Several principles should govern every implementation decision.

**The image remains sovereign.** Overlays support attention; they do not colonize the Field.

**Different grounds deserve different geometries.** A box is not a universal theory of perception.

**Capture can precede naming.** The system must preserve the user’s *this* before demanding a concept.

**Every abstraction retains a path back to evidence.** No Percept or Reading should become detached rhetoric.

**Recall restores force, not merely metadata.** A title in search results is insufficient; the visual nucleus must return.

**The periphery invites; the centre belongs to the current act.** Contextual suggestions should gather at the margin until called.

**Recognition must become learned and immediate.** Stable ground signatures matter more than decorative novelty.

**The archive must permit difference.** Retrieval should find resonance, contrast, and transformation—not only similarity.

**The user’s seeing has a history.** Contradiction and revision are intellectual value, not database mess.

## The motive: from storing vision to continuing it

The great promise of Chiasm is not that it can annotate more kinds of image content. It is that it can resist the usual death of perception inside software.

Most systems convert seeing into information and call the operation complete. The image becomes assets, labels, embeddings, captions, and searchable objects. What disappears is the event by which something came to matter: the hesitation before a shadow, the attraction of an interval, the obscure pressure exerted by a material, the sudden recognition that two distant images are secretly organized by the same gesture.

Chiasm should preserve not only what was seen but **the route by which it became seeable**.

This is the meaning of embodiedness in the project. A percept is embodied when it has somewhere to appear, a manner of appearing, an action it affords, a trace it leaves, and a way of returning. A region is embodied when it is not merely coordinate data but a responsive aperture in the Field. A ground is embodied when its own mode—bounded, distributed, relational, kinetic, atmospheric—is rendered without distortion. Writing becomes embodied when its words can reopen the perceptual world that nourished them.

The aim is not to trap vision in a schema. It is to construct an interface in which perception can continue its life: from image to gesture, from gesture to Percept, from Percept to sentence, from sentence to structure, and from structure back into a newly altered act of seeing.

Then the archive is no longer behind the user. It surrounds the present act as a field of possible return.

And the system is no longer merely remembering images.

It is learning how a person sees.
