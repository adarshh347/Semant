# HW-C5 — Seed intake pack

**A practical intake pack for you, the vault owner. Research-only: nothing here touches code, data,
schema or the database. No books were opened to write it. It is the operational companion to
HW-C4 — that document argues *what* to get and *why*; this one tells you *how* to gather, name,
and hand things over so they are actually usable. Under ten minutes to read; the checklist at the
end is the whole thing in one page.**

---

## Why this matters now

The corpus looks like 127 images. It isn't. Real unclaimed fixture stock is **39** — about 80 posts
are your own sketchbook archive descending from only **10** Instagram carousels, i.e. same book,
same desk, same lamp, minutes apart. Those are near-duplicates, not samples.

So a genuinely new photograph is worth far more than the numbers suggest. Twenty new images would
be a ~50% enlargement of the usable pool.

The single biggest gap is **a photograph of ordinary people in ordinary space**. In 127 posts there
is not one photograph of more than two living people. Every "gesture" we have is carved stone, and
the model's recorded failure — reading a confrontational gaze into a facade with no face — cannot
be *measured* until it has been shown real faces to contrast against.

Texts are useful but never blocking. **Images are the bottleneck.**

## The image list — 12 categories

Priorities: **HIGH** = do these; **MEDIUM** = if convenient; **LOW** = only if it falls in your lap.

### 1. Ordinary people in ordinary space — 3 — **HIGH** (do this first)
Three or more people, faces visible, doing something unremarkable: a bus stop, a queue, a family at
a table, a classroom.
**Good:** a snapshot. **Bad:** a posed group photo, a portrait, anything lit or composed for effect.
**Unlocks:** R7 (its only honest home), R1, R9, R12.

### 2. Clean non-figurative surface — 3–4 — **HIGH**
Concrete, water, sand, bark, plaster, a stain. Fill the frame: no object, no horizon, no figure.
Include **one repeating pattern with a break or defect** (a cracked tile run, a missed stitch) and
**one macro texture at ambiguous scale**.
**Good:** you cannot tell what made it. **Bad:** anything with a manufactured edge in shot.
**Unlocks:** R4 (its home), R12, R8, R5.

### 3. Everyday manufactured objects — 3 — **HIGH**
One clean object on a neutral ground (kettle, chair, tool, phone); one **worn/used** (scuffed shoe,
chipped mug); one **whose function you cannot guess from its shape**.
**Good:** plain light, no styling. **Bad:** a product shot with a gradient backdrop.
**Unlocks:** R9, R12, R1. (This family currently has *zero* usable fixtures.)

### 4. Actual photographed cloth — 2–3 — **HIGH**
Real fabric, not carved fabric: folded textile, a loom, a clothes rack, a worn garment on a body.
Plus **one flat patterned textile with no body** (rug, printed length, embroidery sample).
**Good:** weave or nap legible. **Bad:** drapery on a statue — we have nothing but that.
**Unlocks:** R8 (strongest fit in the atlas), R5, R4.

### 5. A painting the model cannot name — 1–2 — **HIGH**
Full canvas, frame visible, brushwork legible, shot straight on. Local gallery, a relative's wall,
a student show.
**Good:** genuinely unfamous. **Bad:** anything canonical (recall trap), or a tight crop.
**Unlocks:** R2, R9, R5, R12.

### 6. Real map, plan or schematic — 2–3 — **HIGH**
An actual map; an architectural plan or section; a chart/wiring diagram/timetable **with a legend**.
Bonus: one **hybrid** — an annotated photograph, or a plan over a site view.
**Good:** printed labels sharp enough to read. **Bad:** a decorative "vintage map" poster.
**Unlocks:** R5, R1, R9. (Structure-blindness is the highest-scoring risk we have and we own no map.)

### 7. Unceremonial architecture — 2–3 — **MEDIUM–HIGH**
A housing block, a stairwell, a corridor, a **scaffolded or half-built building**, and the valuable
one: an **interior with people actually using it** (station concourse, market hall).
**Good:** boring. **Bad:** anything monumental — that's all we have.
**Unlocks:** R1, R8, R12, R4.

### 8. Ignoble decay — 2–3 — **MEDIUM–HIGH**
Rust, peeling paint, mould, a rotting fence. Plus **fresh damage** (a break, a crack, graffiti) as
opposed to slow weathering. Best of all: an **intact-vs-damaged pair of the same thing**.
**Good:** entropy you cannot mourn. **Bad:** weathered carved stone — we have a whole cemetery.
**Unlocks:** R8, R2, R4, R5.

### 9. Sculpture out of the reverent frame — 2 — **MEDIUM**
Either **abstract / non-figurative** (a metal plate, a public abstract piece) or a figure in a
**non-reverent setting**: a park statue, a shop mannequin, a garden gnome. Also useful: any
sculpture **from behind**, or a fragment with no head.
**Good:** no plinth, no vitrine, no gallery light. **Bad:** museum interior.
**Unlocks:** R4, R12, R2.

### 10. A mundane threshold, enacted — 1–2 — **MEDIUM**
A doorway, turnstile, gate, checkpoint — **with a person actually crossing it**.
**Good:** mid-stride. **Bad:** an empty ornamental archway; every threshold we have is sacred.
**Unlocks:** R4, R2, R8, R5.

### 11. Landscape or a natural edge — 2 — **MEDIUM**
Shoreline, treeline, horizon, a field edge. There is **no landscape at all** in the corpus.
**Good:** open, unpeopled, no built subject. **Bad:** a scenic viewpoint with a monument in it.
**Unlocks:** R4, R1, R5.

### 12. A bad snapshot, and a photograph of a photograph — 1–2 — **LOW–MEDIUM**
One badly framed / flash-lit / banal picture. One **print on a table or an album page** — the
reflexive case.
**Good:** obviously accidental. **Bad:** anything you'd be pleased with.
**Unlocks:** R2, R5, R12. (Every photograph we have is authored, so photographic *accident* has
never been tested.)

*Cheap bonuses if you pass one:* a **rite in progress with participants** (aarti, procession,
congregation — every religious image we own is empty of worshippers), and a **humble domestic
shrine** or roadside niche.

## Naming and storage

The corpus's actual disease is that nearly every post has **no title, no description and no
catalogue data** — only a source URL, and 79 posts share just 10 of those. Everything below exists
to stop the new material inheriting that.

### Folder layout

```
vault/_Inbox/seed-intake/
  images/
    01-people/           07-architecture-ordinary/
    02-surface/          08-decay-ignoble/
    03-object/           09-sculpture-unreverent/
    04-cloth/            10-threshold/
    05-painting/         11-landscape/
    06-diagram/          12-snapshot/
  texts/
    casey/  warburg/  didi-huberman/  _later/
  _rejected/             # near-duplicates you chose not to submit; keep, don't delete
```

Drop files in and stop. Nothing else is required of you.

### Filename convention

```
<cat>-<slug>-<YYYYMM>-<nn>.<ext>
```

- `<cat>` = the two-digit category number above (so the family is never lost if the file moves)
- `<slug>` = 2–4 lowercase words, hyphenated, describing **what is in the frame**, not what it means
- `<YYYYMM>` = when the photo was *taken* (approximate is fine; use `0000-00` if truly unknown)
- `<nn>` = 01, 02, … within that slug — this is what distinguishes siblings

Examples:
`01-bus-stop-queue-202607-01.jpg`
`02-cracked-tile-run-202607-01.jpg`
`08-rusted-gate-hinge-202603-02.jpg`

A filename that survives being useful later is one where, six months on, you can tell two files
apart **without opening them**. If two names differ only by `<nn>`, that is a warning sign — see
duplicates below.

### Sidecar note — one per item, five fields max

Same basename, `.md` extension, sitting next to the file. Thirty seconds each. The last field is
the important one and the one no existing post has.

```markdown
---
file: 01-bus-stop-queue-202607-01.jpg
where: Aundh, Pune — bus stop on the main road
when: July 2026
what: five people waiting, one on a phone, midday, shot from the far kerb
unknown: which route; whether anyone in frame knows each other; the building behind them
---
```

"Unknown building, somewhere in Pune, ~2019" is worth enormously more than a blank field: it gives
us something to score the model's naming *against* instead of scoring it against silence.

For a book, the sidecar goes in `texts/<author>/` and `what` is edition + translator; `unknown` can
be "which chapters matter" — that's ours to work out.

### Duplicates — the rule

The corpus already contains a **byte-identical pair imported twice under different ids**, plus ~79
near-duplicate sketchbook siblings descending from 10 carousels. Near-duplicates are worse than
useless: sampling silently returns siblings and inflates confidence in whatever the batch concludes.

Three rules, in order:

1. **One frame per thing, per session.** If you shot eight versions of the same subject in the same
   light in the same minute, submit **one**. Move the rest to `_rejected/` — keep them, just don't
   hand them over.
2. **Change something, or don't submit it.** A second frame of the same subject is only worth having
   if a *variable* changed: different distance, different light, intact vs damaged, before vs after.
   Say which in the sidecar's `what`.
3. **No carousels, no batch exports.** Never drop a whole camera-roll folder or a downloaded album.
   Everything arrives one file at a time, with a name you typed.

If you're unsure whether two files are duplicates: if the same sidecar `what` line describes both,
they are.

## Do not over-ingest whole books — use slices

**What a slice is:** a *theme* or a *locator* — a short paraphrased pressure plus a pointer to where
it lives ("the section on shadow and peripheral vision"). It is **not** the passage itself. The
program's own record format carries a paraphrase and a locator; a short quotation is optional,
usually null, and capped at a few words. The book is referenced by path and hash; it is never copied
into a run.

**Why the program works this way:**

- **Source ventriloquism.** A rehearsal that loads a theorist and then "finds" exactly what that
  theorist predicts has learned nothing — the vocabulary would have produced that description on any
  input. This has a documented precedent in the program: fluent descriptions turned out to be
  inherited rather than observed. A loaded theorist is the same failure with better prose.
- **Copyright.** Bulk-ingesting a copyrighted book into a research corpus is not something we do.

**What to hand over instead of a whole book:** just tell us where the file is. A path in
`~/Documents/books/`, or a physical book on a shelf with the edition noted, is completely sufficient.
The program takes its own short slices, and records the locator so anyone can go find the passage and
re-derive the pressure independently. You never need to extract, excerpt, transcribe or summarise
anything.

**And the standing rule that makes texts non-urgent:** *no text enters a family until that family has
had one blind run the text could contradict.* Text after baseline, never before. Buy the three books
when convenient; the images are what is actually blocking work.

## What NOT to bring yet

- **Not Lacan** — would make R7 findings unattributable by construction (see above).
- **Not Deleuze** (*Logic of Sensation*) — the vocabulary fits every distorted body and therefore
  discriminates nothing.
- **Not more phenomenology** — the shelf is already saturated at its most absorbent end. What we need
  next is a text that can *lose*.
- **Not Pallasmaa again on the haptic axis** — run 001 spent him; a rerun risks the program echoing
  itself and calling the echo convergence.
- **Not more famous artworks** — canonical images are recall traps; the model answers from memory
  instead of pixels. Unknown beats good.
- **Not more sketchbook pages** — ~75 of the 80 are already excluded from any sampling frame.
- **Not bulk folders, camera rolls, carousels or scraped albums** — volume is not the problem.
- **Not anything you had to seek out artistically.** For categories 1, 2, 8 and 12, effort actively
  makes the image worse.
