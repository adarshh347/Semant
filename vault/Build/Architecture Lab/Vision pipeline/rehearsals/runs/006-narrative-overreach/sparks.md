## spark-08 — the evidence base grows silently to meet the claim

**Origin:** A5, stage 1.

**Observation.** Asked only what is happening in a photograph, the model supplied a title, a
sculptor, a date, an institution, and a **quoted scriptural inscription** — then wrote that the
inscription "confirm[s] the religious context and location." The inscription is not in the image;
the medallion carries a fragmentary non-English band, verified at 6× upscale. The location and
attribution appear confabulated rather than retrieved.

**Enablement claim.** Semant's percepts cite Grounds, and a Ground points at a region of *this*
image. There is no way to record that a reading was licensed by something **not in the frame** —
a remembered attribution, a catalogue fact, a half-recalled monument type. When such a licence is
false, the percept looks exactly like one grounded in the picture.

**Why this outranks ordinary over-reading.** Every earlier run tested whether the model would say
too much *about what is visible*. A5 shows it will **import** evidence and present it as read off
the image. A citation system that can only point inward cannot represent the difference between
"I see this" and "I know this from elsewhere" — and the second is where the error entered.

**Nearest existing construct:** `Ground`. The gap is the inverse of spark-01: spark-01 wanted to
say something about the artifact rather than the depiction; spark-08 wants to mark a claim as
resting on something **outside the image entirely**.

**Deliberately NOT concluded:** that Semant should add an external-citation entity. One run. And
the honest first move is much smaller — a rehearsal convention that records, verbatim, whenever a
model supplies a name, date, place or quotation that the frame does not contain.

**Next test.** Re-run stage 1 on two further monuments of recognisable *type* but no legible text.
If attribution appears each time, this is a standing behaviour, not an accident of one famous pose.

## spark-07 — STRENGTHENED: the counterexample can appear inside a single response

**Origin:** A4 found it across two calls; A5 found it within one.

Stage 1 asserted *"created by Paul Manship in 1921"* and, in the same response's *what the
photograph does not let you say* section, stated *"we cannot determine the sculptor or year from
the image alone."*

**What this adds.** In A4 the contradiction could be explained away — two prompts, a stateless
model, no memory between calls. That explanation is unavailable here. The epistemic guard was
present, was answered **correctly**, and had **no authority** over the claim already made.

**Sharpened claim.** Scope is not the only axis. A statement of what cannot be known does not
retract what was asserted; the two simply coexist. If Semant ever surfaces both a claim and its
own limits, **placement will not be enough** — the limit must be able to reach back.

**Still not concluded:** that Semant should detect contradictions between percepts. Two runs, one
model.

## Withheld

- **Any claim about what this monument actually is** — sculptor, date, cemetery, city. The model
  supplied several; the rehearsal verified none and asserts none. Recording the model's attribution
  as *possibly false* is the finding; asserting the truth would repeat the error under better
  manners.
- **"The angel's grief fills the rotunda."** The sentence under test. It remains unsupported by the
  frame regardless of how persuasively stage 2 endorsed it.
- **Any inference from the medallion's lettering.** Fragmentary and non-English; not read.

## Unresolved questions

1. Is the attribution confabulated or retrieved-and-misapplied? Distinguishing them needs external
   verification this rehearsal deliberately did not perform.
2. Would the model still accept the sentence if the fabricated scaffold were absent — e.g. on a
   monument with no recognisable type?
3. Does third-party attribution actually lower the cost of dissent, as the sycophancy control
   assumes? A5 cannot tell: the model never dissented, so the control was never exercised.
