## 1. Required thinkers

### Merleau-Ponty
- **archive presence: PRESENT (primary + 2 secondary).** `Phenomenology-of-Perception-by-Maurice-Merleau-Ponty.pdf`; supporting: `Merleau-Ponty's Poetic of the World…pdf`, `Phenomenology and the Arts…epub`.
- **perceptual pressure.** He makes the curator notice that the perceiver is *inside* the scene, not
  in front of it — that a body has a position, a horizon, and a set of things it cannot see from
  where it stands. Applied to a photograph this is corrosive in a useful way: it forces the question
  of whose standing-point the image encodes, and what the frame has amputated. He also makes
  depth stop being a property of the picture and start being a relation.
- **families.** **R4 Figure/Ground Reversal** (ground is not a leftover; it is the field a figure
  is carved out of). **R2 Sensitisation-and-Return** (the second look is his native move — perception
  as revisable, not as retrieval). **R8 Surface Becoming Structure** (the visible always trails an
  invisible). Deliberately *not* R7, per the existing A3 decision.
- **slice themes.** (a) the chiasm of touching and being touched; (b) the horizon as what makes a
  thing a thing rather than a patch; (c) depth as the "first" dimension, not the derived one;
  (d) the body as the zero-point of orientation.
- **ventriloquism risk: HIGH.** His vocabulary is so absorbent that any image can be re-described in
  it, and the re-description always succeeds — which means it never tests anything. VLMs are also
  saturated with second-hand phenomenology and will produce it on the faintest cue.
  **Guard:** run blind first; introduce the text only on a second pass, and require that the second
  pass name at least one thing the blind pass got *wrong*, not merely something it missed. If the
  post-text score contains no correction, discard the text pass.
- **user must supply?** No.

### Heidegger
- **archive presence: ABSENT.** No primary text. Only indirect discussion inside
  `Van Gogh Among the Philosophers` and possibly `Phenomenology and the Arts`.
- **perceptual pressure.** He would make a curator notice the difference between a thing that is
  *in use* and a thing that has been lifted out of use into display — the museum plinth as an
  ontological event. He also supplies the sharpest available warning about the rehearsal's own
  temptation: the leap from a depicted worn object to a whole imagined world of labour.
- **families.** **R9 Narrative Overreach** — he is arguably the best *negative* specimen available,
  because the shoes essay is the canonical case of a philosopher over-reading a painting, and the
  Schapiro/Derrida rebuttal is the canonical correction. **R8 Surface Becoming Structure** (equipment
  withdrawing into use; breakdown making structure visible).
- **slice themes.** (a) equipment withdrawing into use until it breaks; (b) the artwork setting up a
  world versus the object sitting in a display case; (c) the peasant-shoes attribution and its
  refutation — overreach as a documented event.
- **ventriloquism risk: HIGH.** Twice over: the idiom is hypnotic, and the specific text is a proven
  vehicle for confident projection onto an image. **Guard:** if used at all, use him as the *subject*
  of R9 rather than its instrument — load the shoes controversy as a case study of failure, never as
  an interpretive frame for a new image.
- **user must supply?** **Yes** — if wanted. Recommended acquisition is not *Being and Time* but the
  Origin of the Work of Art essay, ideally in an edition that carries the Schapiro/Derrida dispute.

### Juhani Pallasmaa
- **archive presence: PRESENT (two editions + a second book).** `The Eyes of the Skin` PDF (2005) and
  EPUB (2012); `The Thinking Hand` (2009).
- **perceptual pressure.** He makes a curator notice temperature, weight, grain, and the hand's
  memory — the sensory registers a photograph solicits but cannot deliver. His real utility in this
  program is that he *reliably generates evidence debt*: every haptic claim he prompts is a claim
  the image cannot settle, which is exactly the productive failure run 001 recorded.
- **families.** **R5 Sensory Disagreement** (his central subject is the quarrel between the eye and
  the rest). **R8 Surface Becoming Structure** (material as time turned into shape). **R2** for
  return under a haptic pressure.
- **slice themes.** (a) the shadow as an invitation to peripheral vision; (b) material as sedimented
  duration; (c) the hand that thinks rather than merely grips; (d) imagining an object's unseen back
  and displaced air.
- **ventriloquism risk: MEDIUM, and partly *spent*.** Medium because his claims are affective and
  hard to falsify; partly spent because run 001 already used him, so a second run risks *self*-
  ventriloquism — the program echoing its own first move. **Guard:** if reused, switch book (use
  *The Thinking Hand*, not *Eyes of the Skin*) and switch family, so the pressure is not a rerun.
- **user must supply?** No.

### Edward Casey
- **archive presence: PARTIAL / effectively ABSENT.** Only as co-editor of
  `Adventures in Phenomenology: Gaston Bachelard`. No Casey monograph.
- **perceptual pressure.** Casey would make a curator distinguish *place* from *space* — and, more
  usefully here, distinguish the edge of a thing from the edge of the frame. His work on rims,
  boundaries and horizons is the sharpest tool available for R4, because it gives vocabulary for the
  ground as an *implacement* rather than as background wash.
- **families.** **R4 Figure/Ground Reversal** (primary fit — boundary, rim, edge). **R1 Open
  Constellation** (place as gathering; how several images can constitute one locale).
- **slice themes.** (a) rim versus boundary versus border; (b) place as gathering rather than as
  container; (c) body memory as the medium of implacement; (d) the horizon as the outer edge of a
  place, not of a picture.
- **ventriloquism risk: LOW–MEDIUM.** His distinctions are unusually operational — you can check
  whether an image has a rim — which makes them testable rather than merely restatable.
- **user must supply?** **Yes.** *Getting Back into Place* or *The Fate of Place*. This is the
  highest-value phenomenology acquisition precisely because it is the least ventriloquisable.

### Manuel DeLanda
- **archive presence: PARTIAL.** `Philosophy and Simulation` only — his emergence/simulation book,
  not the assemblage or morphogenesis texts.
- **perceptual pressure.** He is the archive's one anti-phenomenologist, and that is his value. He
  makes a curator ask what *process* produced a form — settling, erosion, casting, tooling, wear —
  rather than what the form *means*. Under DeLanda, a surface is an output of a generative history,
  and the question shifts from significance to mechanism.
- **families.** **R8 Surface Becoming Structure** (primary — form as the residue of a process).
  **R4** (figure and ground as differently-scaled populations rather than as subject and backdrop).
- **slice themes.** (a) emergence from lower-level interaction rather than from design; (b)
  topological versus metric description of form; (c) the population-thinking move — a form as one
  outcome of a distribution; (d) simulation as an epistemic device, with its own limits.
- **ventriloquism risk: LOW.** His claims are mechanistic and therefore embarrassable by the image:
  either the surface bears process evidence or it does not. He is a *corrective* against the
  phenomenological drift of the rest of the shelf and should be favoured for that reason.
- **user must supply?** No for R8 work; **yes** if assemblage vocabulary is wanted
  (*Assemblage Theory* / *A Thousand Years of Nonlinear History*).

### Gaston Bachelard
- **archive presence: PRESENT.** `The Poetics of Space` (Beacon 1994); plus the Casey-co-edited
  `Adventures in Phenomenology` as secondary.
- **perceptual pressure.** He makes a curator notice interiority, nesting, and the scale-jumping
  reverie an image licenses — corners, drawers, shells, the felt difference between a hollow and a
  volume. He is also the clearest case of a text that *invites* the exact move the program is trying
  to police: he treats imaginative amplification as a virtue.
- **families.** **R9 Narrative Overreach** — as calibration material, because he shows what
  full-throttle amplification sounds like when it is deliberate and owned. **R1 Open Constellation**
  (images gathering by resonance rather than by category).
- **slice themes.** (a) the nest and the shell as figures of inhabited roundness; (b) the corner as
  a refuge with its own phenomenology; (c) intimate immensity — the small opening onto the vast;
  (d) reverberation versus mere resonance in the reception of an image.
- **ventriloquism risk: HIGH.** He does not merely permit projection, he *rewards* it, and a VLM
  given Bachelard will produce beautiful unfalsifiable prose on any input. **Guard:** use only
  inside R9, where overreach is the object of study, and require the score to mark every Bachelardian
  articulation as a Reading with a named evidence debt — never as an observation.
- **user must supply?** No.

### Steven Holl
- **archive presence: PRESENT (two).** `Parallax` (2001); `Steven Holl: Watercolors` (2002).
- **perceptual pressure.** Holl makes a curator notice that a view is one of many, and that moving
  changes what is true of the object — parallax as the discipline of never trusting a single
  station-point. *Watercolors* is an unusual asset: it is a record of an architect thinking *through*
  a medium, useful for asking what a rendering technique adds and subtracts.
- **families.** **R4 Figure/Ground Reversal** (overlap and occlusion change with position).
  **R2 Sensitisation-and-Return** (return as literal re-positioning, not just re-reading).
  **R5** for the watercolour/photograph medium quarrel.
- **slice themes.** (a) parallax — the object reorganised by the moving observer; (b) the
  chromatic space of a passage rather than of a room; (c) porosity and enmeshed experience;
  (d) drawing as a mode of thinking, not of recording.
- **ventriloquism risk: LOW.** His terms make positional, checkable claims; a still photograph
  denies them the movement they require, which produces honest failure rather than fluent agreement.
- **user must supply?** No.

## 2. Proposed gaze/address thinkers — the live need

Context: run 004 found the model answers "where does this gesture's address go?" with a stance
toward *the viewer*, and will attribute address to a facade with no figure in it (`sparks.md`
spark-05). The program currently has **no text at all** for this, by choice. None of the six
candidates below is in the archive.

### Lacan
- **presence: ABSENT.** Not in archive.
- **pressure.** He would make the curator notice that the gaze is not the viewer's property — that
  the picture can be the thing doing the looking, and the viewer the thing caught in the field. This
  directly names run 004's finding: address as a structure of the visual field rather than an
  attribute of a depicted figure.
- **families.** **R7 Gesture and Address** (the exact live need). **R12 Adversarial Projection**
  (the viewer's own investment as the thing being surfaced).
- **slice themes.** (a) the gaze located in the object, not the eye; (b) the anamorphic stain that
  only resolves from a disavowed position; (c) the screen through which the subject is seen;
  (d) the difference between the eye and the gaze.
- **ventriloquism risk: HIGH.** Two compounding failures: the apparatus is self-sealing, and the
  model has been trained on enough Lacanian art-writing to generate it unprompted, so any "finding"
  is unattributable. **Guard:** never load the text; use it only to write *questions* for a blind
  run, and only adopt an answer if the blind run reached it without the vocabulary.
- **user must supply?** **Yes** — Seminar XI, the four chapters on the gaze.

### Barthes
- **presence: ABSENT.**
- **pressure.** He makes the curator separate what a photograph *codes* from what it merely
  *registers*, and gives a defensible name to the detail that snags attention without being the
  subject. He would also force the distinction the program keeps needing: the image as a record of
  a thing that was there, versus the image as a composed statement.
- **families.** **R9 Narrative Overreach** (studium/punctum as a discipline of not narrating).
  **R7** (the photograph's address to its viewer). **R5** (the photograph's flatness against the
  sensory registers it evokes).
- **slice themes.** (a) studium versus punctum — the general interest against the wounding detail;
  (b) the photograph as certificate of presence; (c) the third meaning that exceeds both the
  informational and the symbolic; (d) anchorage — the caption steering the image.
- **ventriloquism risk: MEDIUM.** *Punctum* is dangerously easy to claim and impossible to audit;
  but the studium/punctum split is genuinely useful as a *sorting* device. **Guard:** allow the
  studium half only; require any punctum claim to name the pixel region.
- **user must supply?** **Yes** — *Camera Lucida* is the priority; *Image-Music-Text* secondary.

### Didi-Huberman
- **presence: ABSENT.**
- **pressure.** He would make the curator dwell on what in an image *refuses* to become knowledge —
  the patch, the tear, the blur — and on the fact that looking back at us is a real property of
  certain images rather than a metaphor. He is also the best available theorist of the anachronism
  problem: images carrying times that do not belong to their date.
- **families.** **R7 Gesture and Address** (the image that looks back — precisely run 004's problem).
  **R8 Surface Becoming Structure** (the *pan*, the detail that is not a detail). **R2** (return as
  the constitutive act of looking).
- **slice themes.** (a) what we see looks back at us; (b) the symptom in the image — the place where
  representation tears; (c) anachronism as a method rather than an error; (d) the detail that resists
  being read as a sign.
- **ventriloquism risk: MEDIUM–HIGH.** His prose licenses profundity about any blur. But of the six,
  he is the best *conceptual* fit for the actual live need, and his emphasis on resistance aligns
  with the program's existing `resistances` field. **Guard:** two-pass; the blind pass must nominate
  the resisting region *before* the text is introduced.
- **user must supply?** **Yes** — *Confronting Images*.

### Benjamin / Warburg
- **presence: ABSENT (both).**
- **pressure.** Warburg would make the curator notice gesture as something that *travels* — a pose
  recurring across images, periods, and media, carrying charge with it. That is directly usable: the
  program already compares a subject against neighbours, and Warburg gives that comparison a
  principle other than embedding distance. Benjamin adds the question of what reproduction does to
  the thing — which the program confronts every time it works from a photograph.
- **families.** **R1 Open Constellation** (Warburg's *Mnemosyne* panel is literally a constellation
  method; Benjamin's constellation is the term's source). **R7** (the pathos formula as inherited
  gesture). **R2** (aura and the second look).
- **slice themes.** (a) the pathos formula — gesture surviving its original context; (b) the panel
  as an argument made by juxtaposition; (c) aura and its decay under reproduction; (d) the dialectical
  image — the past legible only at a specific moment.
- **ventriloquism risk: LOW–MEDIUM.** Unusually low for this group, because Warburg's claim is
  *comparative and checkable*: either the pose recurs across the assembled images or it does not.
  Benjamin's aura is softer and should be quarantined from Warburg's method.
- **user must supply?** **Yes.** For method, secondary literature on the *Mnemosyne Atlas* is more
  usable than Warburg's own fragmentary writing.

### Deleuze
- **presence: ABSENT.** (DeLanda in the archive is downstream of Deleuze but is not a substitute.)
- **pressure.** On Bacon, he would make the curator notice the figure as a body under deformation —
  force acting on flesh — and distinguish the figurative from the figural. He supplies a way to talk
  about a distorted or bound body without narrating what happened to it, which is exactly the trap
  run 004's bound-face subject sets.
- **families.** **R8 Surface Becoming Structure** (the diagram; force made visible). **R4** (the
  figure isolated against a flat field — Bacon's ground as an operative armature, not a backdrop).
  **R9** (the figural as an alternative to storytelling).
- **slice themes.** (a) figural versus figurative — sensation without narration; (b) the diagram as
  the imposed catastrophe from which form emerges; (c) forces rendered visible on a body; (d) the
  flat ground as an active field.
- **ventriloquism risk: HIGH.** Terminology-dense, and the vocabulary retrofits onto any distorted
  body with total success and zero discrimination. **Guard:** defer; if used, use only the
  figural/figurative distinction as a *binary sorting question*, and load nothing else.
- **user must supply?** **Yes** — *Francis Bacon: The Logic of Sensation*, not the major works.

### Rancière
- **presence: ABSENT.**
- **pressure.** He would make the curator notice that the choice of what is worth attending to is
  itself the political act — that a rehearsal's framing distributes visibility before any observation
  is made. Applied reflexively, he is a critique of the program rather than a tool inside it: who
  decided this fixture was a subject and that one a "negative"?
- **families.** **R12 Adversarial Projection** (the frame's own commitments as the adversary).
  **R9** (the pensive image that withholds narrative).
- **slice themes.** (a) the distribution of the sensible; (b) the emancipated spectator against the
  spectator-as-passive; (c) the pensive image — a photograph that refuses to resolve into a story;
  (d) the regimes of art as competing framings of the same object.
- **ventriloquism risk: MEDIUM.** Low risk of him "finding" things in the image, because he is
  mostly not about images; higher risk of producing generic institutional critique that is true of
  every rehearsal and therefore informative about none.
- **user must supply?** **Yes** — *The Emancipated Spectator*. Lowest priority of the six.

## 4. The three highest-value acquisitions

1. **Edward Casey — a monograph (*Getting Back into Place* or *The Fate of Place*).**
   The archive currently has him in name only. He is the single lowest-risk / highest-yield gap:
   R4 is a standing family with no dedicated text, and his rim/boundary/edge distinctions make
   *checkable* demands on an image rather than absorbing it. He would also let R4 stop borrowing
   Merleau-Ponty, which is the ventriloquism-heaviest option on the shelf.

2. **Aby Warburg — the *Mnemosyne Atlas* method (secondary literature acceptable, arguably better).**
   This is the only candidate that supplies a *method* rather than a vocabulary. R1 Open Constellation
   currently assembles images without a stated principle of assembly, and cross-image gesture
   comparison is exactly what run 004 needed. Warburgian claims survive or die on whether the pose
   actually recurs — so the text cannot ventriloquise the finding.

3. **Georges Didi-Huberman — *Confronting Images*.**
   The direct answer to the live R7/gaze need, and the only acquisition that gives theoretical
   standing to the program's existing `resistances` field: the part of the image that refuses to
   become knowledge. Preferred over Lacan for the same job because his claims stay at the level of
   the image rather than of a subject-theory, and because he is markedly less pre-installed in a
   VLM's default register.

*Runner-up, deliberately not in the top three:* Barthes' *Camera Lucida*. Cheap, useful, but the
punctum is an audit nightmare and the program does not yet have a way to hold it accountable.

## 5. Thinkers to deliberately NOT use yet — and why

This section is the point of the map. An over-theorised rehearsal produces confident nonsense; the
program's own record already shows what happens when a fluent description is inherited rather than
observed (three consecutive R0 portfolio fixtures were found to be repeating detector output, per
run 004's source notes). A loaded theorist is the same failure mode with better prose.

**Do not use yet — Lacan.** The strongest reason is evidential, not doctrinal: the model already
produces gaze-and-address language *without* any text (spark-05). If Lacan is loaded and the model
then produces Lacanian address, nothing has been learned, because the same output was already
available for free. The finding would be unattributable by construction. Lacan can only become
usable *after* the program has a baseline of unprompted address language to subtract.

**Do not use yet — Deleuze.** The vocabulary succeeds on every distorted body, which means it
discriminates nothing. Under R8 he would generate a "diagram" for any surface. He is also the one
candidate whose adoption would quietly restructure the program's language — force, sensation,
diagram — in a way that is hard to reverse. Defer until R8 has produced findings in plain language
that a Deleuzian reading could then be tested *against*.

**Do not use yet — Heidegger as an interpretive frame.** He may be used as the *object* of an R9
study; he must not be used as its instrument. The shoes essay is the discipline's own worked example
of a philosopher finding in a painting exactly what his framework required. Loading it as a lens
would reproduce the error the family exists to detect.

**Do not re-use yet — Pallasmaa on the same axis.** Run 001 already spent him. A second haptic
rehearsal on *Eyes of the Skin* risks the program echoing itself and mistaking the echo for
convergence. If he returns, it should be *The Thinking Hand* under a different family.

**Handle Bachelard only inside R9.** He is the best available demonstration of amplification done
knowingly, and the worst available frame for an observation. Outside R9 he should not be loaded at all.

**Actively prefer, as counterweight: DeLanda and Holl.** Both make claims a photograph can refuse.
The archive is heavily phenomenological, and the correct next textual move is probably not to add
more phenomenology but to load a text that can *lose*.

**A general rule this map proposes:** no text should enter a family until that family has produced
at least one blind run whose findings the text could contradict. Text after baseline, never before.

## 6. How a text should enter a rehearsal

Established by run 001 and encoded in
`schemas/virtual-rehearsal-score.schema.json` → `$defs/source_pressure`.

- **Pressure, not authority.** The `use` field is a const: `"pressure_not_authority"`. A text may
  make the curator ask something; it may never settle what an image means, and it can never
  establish cultural, devotional, political, funerary or iconographic content. Run 001's source
  notes state this explicitly for the Pallasmaa case.
- **The record shape.** Required: `pressure_id`, `source_ref`, `locator`, `paraphrase`, `use`.
  Optional and nullable: `brief_quote`. `additionalProperties: false` — nothing else may be smuggled in.
  The `paraphrase` is the load-bearing field; `brief_quote` is an orientation aid and should usually
  be `null`.
- **Paraphrase over quotation.** One short sub-15-word quote per pressure at most, and only where a
  paraphrase would lose the operative distinction. Run 001's two quotes are five and four words. The
  book itself is referenced by **path and hash in `manifest.yaml`** and is never copied into the run.
- **Locator, not page dump.** `locator` names a section ("The Significance of the Shadow"), which is
  enough for a curator to go find the passage and re-derive the pressure independently.
- **`source_pressures: []` is a legitimate, recordable choice.** Run 004 left it empty on purpose and
  said so in its source notes. An empty array with a stated reason is stronger evidence than a
  populated one without.
- **Preferred protocol going forward: two-pass.** Blind run first, text on the second pass, and the
  second pass must be able to name what the first got wrong. If the text pass only *adds* fluency,
  the text did no work and the pass should be discarded rather than published.
