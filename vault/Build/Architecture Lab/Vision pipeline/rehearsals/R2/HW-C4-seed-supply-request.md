# HW-C4 — Seed supply request

**This is a request to you, the vault owner. Research-only: nothing here changes code, data or
schema. It is a shopping list — what to go and find so the next batch of rehearsals can be run
honestly. No books were opened to write it; it synthesises the two Seed Ecology inventories
(HW-L2 images, HW-L3 texts). Five minutes to read, then it is actionable.**

## Lead with this one

**A photograph of ordinary people in ordinary space.** Three or more people, a street, a platform,
a market, a waiting room, a kitchen. Taken with **no artistic intent** — a snapshot is better than
a good picture.

Why it is first, ahead of everything else:

- It is the **only** way to run **R7 Gesture and Address** honestly. The corpus contains *zero*
  photographs of more than two living people. Every "gesture" fixture we have is carved stone.
- It fills **crowd/assembly**, the sole family currently marked `SHOULD WAIT`.
- It is the **missing control for the anthropomorphism defect.** The recorded failure is that the
  model reads a gaze into a figureless facade (`695be843`: "windows that function as eyes… a
  confrontational gaze"). Until it is shown real faces and real address, projection can be
  *observed* but never *measured* — we have the failure and no contrast case.
- It breaks the corpus's uniform register. All 127 posts are museum, monument, cemetery, church, or
  your own lamp-lit desk. Reverence is currently indistinguishable from perception.

One good image changes the program. Three would be better.

**Also worth knowing before you shop:** the corpus is not 127 usable images. Real unclaimed fixture
stock is **39**. About 80 posts are your own sketchbook archive, descending from only **10**
Instagram carousels — same book, same desk, same lamp, minutes apart. They are near-duplicates, not
samples. So a handful of genuinely new images moves the needle more than it sounds like it should.

## Top 8 image types to bring

### 1. Ordinary people in ordinary space — **bring 3**
Three or more people in a mundane setting, faces visible, doing something unremarkable. Good: a bus
stop, a queue, a family at a table, a classroom. Bad: a posed group photo, a portrait, anything
composed or lit for effect — that puts us back in "authored photograph", which we already have.
**Why:** the anthropomorphism control, above. **Unlocks:** R7 (its natural home), plus R1, R9, R12.

### 2. A clean non-figurative surface — **bring 3–4**
A surface with nothing made in it: concrete, water, sand, bark, a plaster wall, a stain. Fill the
frame; no object, no horizon, no figure. Include **one repeating pattern with a break or defect in
it** (a cracked tile run, a missed stitch) and **one macro texture at ambiguous scale**.
**Why:** this is the family with the **highest anthropomorphism exposure of all twelve**, and there
is not one image in the corpus without a made object in it. The defect case is the sharpest test we
have of whether the model reports what is there or silently completes the pattern.
**Unlocks:** R4 Figure/Ground Reversal (its home), R12, R8, R5.

### 3. Everyday manufactured objects — **bring 3**
One clean object on a neutral ground (a kettle, a chair, a tool, a phone); one **worn/used** object
(a scuffed shoe, a chipped mug); one object **whose function you cannot guess from its shape**.
Plain light, no styling.
**Why:** object/design is the weakest family in the atlas — it has *no* usable fixtures. Any object
rehearsal today would run on sculpture-in-a-vitrine, and the model would import ritual register into
what should be plain description. The unguessable object is the cleanest possible overreach test.
**Unlocks:** R9 Narrative Overreach, R12, R1.

### 4. Actual photographed cloth — **bring 2–3**
Real fabric, not carved fabric: a folded textile, a loom, a rack of clothing, a worn garment on a
body. Plus **one flat textile with pattern and no body** — a rug, a printed length, an embroidery
sample.
**Why:** there is **zero photographed cloth in 127 posts** — every "textile" we have is stone
drapery. The annotator already emits `dress` / `scarf` / `tie knot` for an oil-painting crop, i.e.
it invents garment vocabulary from tonal shapes. Real cloth is how we find out whether it can tell
the difference. **Unlocks:** R8 Surface Becoming Structure (strongest fit in the atlas), R5, R4.

### 5. A painting the model cannot name — **bring 1–2**
A full canvas, frame visible, brushwork legible, photographed straight on. It must be **unfamous** —
a local gallery, a relative's wall, a student show. Bad: anything canonical (the corpus already has
the Mona Lisa, which is a pure recall trap), and bad: a tight crop.
**Why:** every painting in the corpus is either famous or generic, so we cannot separate looking
from recall. Domain confidence is also provably unreliable here in both directions — a pencil
drawing was called "painting" at 0.98. **Unlocks:** R2 Sensitisation-and-Return, R9, R5, R12.

### 6. A real map, plan or schematic — **bring 2–3**
An actual map (any kind); an architectural plan or section; a chart/wiring diagram/timetable **with
a legend**. Ideally one **hybrid** too — an annotated photograph, or a plan overlaid on a site view.
**Why:** the 002 failure was exactly this family — a labelled comparative plate read as a "collage".
Structure-blindness is the highest-scoring risk in the atlas, and we have no map and no plan at all.
Expect it to read printed labels as objects in the scene; we need material to prove that.
**Unlocks:** R5 Sensory Disagreement (a diagram is a picture that refuses sensory reading), R1, R9.

### 7. Unceremonial architecture — **bring 2–3**
A housing block, a stairwell, a corridor, a **building under construction or scaffolded**, and — the
valuable one — an **interior with people actually using it** (station concourse, market hall).
**Why:** every architectural image in the corpus is a monument, which biases the model toward
reverent language *before it has looked*. Bilaterally symmetric monumental facades are also exactly
the face-pareidolia stimulus. Scaffolding denies the model its "completed masterpiece" framing.
**Unlocks:** R1 Open Constellation, R8, R12, R4.

### 8. Ignoble decay — **bring 2–3**
Rust, peeling paint, mould on a wall, a rotting fence. Plus **fresh damage** (a break, a crack,
graffiti) as opposed to slow weathering. If you can, an **intact-vs-damaged pair of the same thing**.
**Why:** all decay in the corpus is *noble* decay on carved stone, and it pulls the model into
elegy — it asserts "anguished", "weeping" from erosion noise. We need entropy the model cannot
mourn. **Unlocks:** R8, R2, R4, R5.

*Cheap bonus if you pass one:* a **mundane threshold with a person crossing it** — a doorway, a
turnstile, a gate. Every threshold in the corpus is sacred or accidental, and none is enacted.

## Top 5 text / theorist gaps

Context you should have: the archive is **12 files and almost entirely phenomenological** — i.e.
weighted toward the most ventriloquisable end of the shelf. Merleau-Ponty, Pallasmaa ×3, Bachelard,
Holl ×2, partial DeLanda. **Casey is present in name only** (his folder holds a Bachelard volume he
co-edited). Heidegger and all six gaze/address candidates are absent.

### 1. Edward Casey — a monograph. **PRIORITY NOW.**
*Getting Back into Place* or *The Fate of Place*.
**Pressure:** makes you distinguish the edge of a *thing* from the edge of the *frame* — rim,
boundary, border — and treat the ground as a place rather than as leftover background.
**Unlocks:** R4 (which currently has no dedicated text and is borrowing Merleau-Ponty, the
heaviest ventriloquism risk on the shelf), R1.
**Why now:** lowest risk, highest yield. His distinctions make *checkable* demands on an image —
you can ask whether an image has a rim and be wrong.

### 2. Aby Warburg — the *Mnemosyne Atlas* method. **PRIORITY NOW.**
Secondary literature is fine, arguably better than his own fragmentary writing.
**Pressure:** gesture as something that *travels* — the same pose recurring across images, periods
and media, carrying charge with it.
**Unlocks:** R1 Open Constellation, R7.
**Why now:** the only candidate that supplies a **method** rather than a vocabulary. R1 currently
assembles images with no stated principle of assembly beyond embedding distance. And Warburgian
claims survive or die on whether the pose actually recurs — so the text cannot ventriloquise the
finding.

### 3. Georges Didi-Huberman — *Confronting Images*. **PRIORITY NOW.**
**Pressure:** what in an image *refuses* to become knowledge — the patch, the tear, the blur — and
the claim that certain images looking back at us is a property, not a metaphor.
**Unlocks:** R7 (the live gaze/address need), R8, R2.
**Why now:** the direct answer to the R7 problem, and the only acquisition that gives theoretical
standing to the `resistances` field we already have. Preferred over Lacan because he stays at the
level of the image and is far less pre-installed in a model's default register.

### 4. Heidegger — *The Origin of the Work of Art*. **LATER.**
Ideally an edition carrying the Schapiro/Derrida dispute over the Van Gogh shoes.
**Pressure:** the difference between a thing *in use* and a thing lifted out of use into display —
the museum plinth as an event.
**Unlocks:** R9 Narrative Overreach — but as its **specimen**, not its instrument. The shoes essay
is the discipline's own worked example of a philosopher finding in a painting exactly what his
framework required. Buy it as a case study of failure; never load it as a lens.
**Later:** not *Being and Time*, and not urgent.

### 5. Roland Barthes — *Camera Lucida*. **LATER.**
**Pressure:** separates what a photograph *codes* from what it merely *registers*, and names the
detail that snags attention without being the subject.
**Unlocks:** R9, R7, R5.
**Later:** cheap and useful, but *punctum* is an audit nightmare — impossible to falsify — and the
program has no way yet to hold it accountable. Deliberately kept out of the top three.

## What NOT to bring yet — and why this matters as much as the asks

- **Do not buy Lacan.** This is evidential, not taste. The model **already produces gaze and
  address language with no text loaded** (spark-05). If we load Lacan and it then produces Lacanian
  address, nothing has been learned — the same output was available for free. The finding would be
  unattributable by construction. Lacan becomes usable only after we have a baseline of unprompted
  address language to subtract. Please do not helpfully supply him early.
- **Do not buy Deleuze** (*Logic of Sensation*) yet. The vocabulary retrofits onto any distorted
  body with total success and zero discrimination, and adopting it would quietly restructure the
  program's language in a way that is hard to reverse.
- **Do not buy more phenomenology.** The shelf is already saturated at exactly its most absorbent
  end. The correct next textual move is a text that can **lose** — DeLanda and Holl are already the
  useful counterweights we have.
- **Do not re-use Pallasmaa on the haptic axis.** Run 001 spent him; a second haptic pass risks the
  program echoing itself and calling the echo convergence.
- **Do not bring more famous artworks.** Canonical images are recall traps — the model answers from
  memory instead of pixels. Unknown beats good.
- **Do not bring more sketchbook pages.** ~75 of the existing 80 are already excluded from any
  sampling frame; more would inflate confidence, not evidence.
- **Standing rule:** no text enters a family until that family has had one blind run the text could
  contradict. Text after baseline, never before. So texts are useful but never blocking — **images
  are the bottleneck.**

## Please bring provenance

Nearly every post in the corpus currently has **no title, no description and no catalogue data** —
only a source URL, and 79 of them share just 10. When you add images, attach whatever you actually
know, even if it is scrappy:

- **where** it was taken or found (place, or the URL/book/gallery)
- **when** (a year is enough)
- **what** it is, in one plain sentence — and, importantly, **what you do not know**

A note saying "unknown building, somewhere in Pune, ~2019" is worth far more than a blank field: it
lets us score the model's naming against something instead of against silence.
