# Reading, not tagging

Computers are very good at tagging an image. *Person. Dress. Handbag. 94% confidence.* An entire industry runs on it — search, catalogs, SEO, trend dashboards counting how many times a colour appears this season. All of it answers the same small question: **what is in the picture?**

That question has almost nothing to do with why a picture matters.

An image doesn't move you because it *contains* a dress. It moves you because of how the dress falls, what the light withholds, the quiet argument between the figure and the room behind her. Meaning doesn't live in the objects. It lives in the parts, the textures, the relations — the things a label can't hold.

Darshan reads instead of tags. It treats an image the way a critic treats a poem: it decomposes it into meaningful parts, and then it *reads each part* through lenses the image itself calls for — its silhouette, its drape, its era, the way it stages a feeling. Where a tagger flattens the picture to a list, Darshan opens it up into an interpretation you can disagree with, extend, and write from.

This is the oldest intellectual act there is — close reading — pointed at the thing culture actually lives in now: the image, the reel, the scroll. It's how you turn *looking* into *thinking*, and thinking into something you can say.

Tagging tells you what you're looking at. Reading tells you what you're seeing. We only care about the second one.
