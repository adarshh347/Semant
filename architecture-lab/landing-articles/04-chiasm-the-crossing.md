# Chiasm — the crossing

There is a moment, when you are looking at something and trying to say why it holds you, where seeing and saying stop being two acts. The eye finds the fold; the sentence reaches for it; and for an instant you cannot tell which came first. The philosopher Merleau-Ponty had a word for that intertwining — the *chiasm* — the crossing where the one who sees and the thing seen fold into each other. He thought it was the deepest fact about perception: that we are not outside the world looking in, but woven into it, seer and seen exchanging places.

Most software keeps them apart. The image lives on one side, a source you consult. The words live on the other, where the "real" thinking supposedly happens. You look left, you type right, and the detail that moved you — the actual fall of the fabric — never leaves the picture. It stays behind glass, and your sentence describes a memory of it.

Semant is built on the opposite conviction. Its workspace has two shores — the **Field**, where the image is seen and taken apart by attention, and the **Manuscript**, where it becomes language — but the product is neither shore. It is the crossing between them. A part you notice in the Field does not merely get *linked* to your writing. It travels. The shoulder drape you marked becomes a live object inside the sentence — hover it and the shape lights up on the image; write about it and the image remembers being written about. Seeing looks forward into the argument; the argument looks back at what it is answering to.

This is why the workspace is called Chiasm. Not as decoration — as architecture. The whole system is arranged so a fragment of the visible world can cross into thought and stay alive on both sides at once.

And it points at something larger than a nicer editor. For a decade the frontier in machines has been *context* — how to give a model the right ground to stand on so its words mean something. We have engineered context out of documents, databases, embeddings. Semant proposes a different substrate: the **percept** — the exact fragment of the seen that entered a human's attention, carrying its shape, its reading, its history of being noticed. Writing grounded in percepts is not autocomplete guessing the next sentence. It is language answering to the world it came from, with the evidence attached.

That is the new frontier we are building toward — not a tool that describes images, but a medium where perception and composition finally cross.
