# Saving is not seeing

We have never had more places to *save* an image. Pinterest, Cosmos, Are.na, Savee, a thousand screenshots you will never open again. The tools have become extraordinary at hoarding beauty and useless at understanding it. You save a look, a room, a frame — and the *reason* you saved it, the thing your eye actually caught, evaporates the second you scroll on.

That reason is the only part that matters.

A moodboard is an archive of your attention with the attention removed. It remembers *that* you stopped; it forgets *why*. And so the boards pile up — hundreds of blocks, no memory of what any of them meant — until you're drowning in references and can't say what you're actually chasing. Designers call it drift. It's not a lack of inspiration. It's a lack of *seeing*.

Darshan begins where saving ends. It takes one image and slows down inside it — not "a dress, a person, a bag," but the fall of the neckline, the way the light sits on the fabric, the tension a colour holds against the room. It reads the image part by part, and then it asks the only question that builds taste: *how does this move you?* You answer in your own words, and it remembers — not the image, but your reading of it.

Do that a hundred times and something no other tool can give you appears: a **portrait of your own eye**. The motifs you return to. The details you can't stop noticing. Your taste, finally written down as language instead of scattered across boards you'll never revisit.

Saving is a reflex. Seeing is a practice. We built the instrument for the practice.
